Sun-as-a-Star Hack Week

September 8-11, 2020 / Flatiron Institute / New York City

This workshop (inspired by the Gaia Sprints and tess.science workshops) is designed to bring together a group of people with a common interest in utilizing observations of the Sun as a star, including the HARPS-N solar spectra. While a focus of this workshop will be learning lessons from the solar data to apply to extreme precision radial velocity (EPRV) planet searches, we strongly encourage participation from other subfields including heliophysics and stellar physics. This meeting is intended to build new collaborations and encourage experimentation with new ideas and methods. This is not a traditional scientific conference and formal talks will be minimal. Instead, the time will be spent in informal discussions and co-working, with the goal of having tangible results by the end of the week.

Schedule and Location

The Solar Spectroscopy Hack Week will take place from September 8 to September 11, 2020 at the Flatiron Institute in New York City.

Participants can expect to spend the majority of their time working collaboratively. The first day of the workshop will feature some talks focused on building familiarity with features of the available data and previous work done in this area. On all subsequent days, there will be no fixed schedule aside from group check-ins at the beginning and end of each day.

Collaboration Policy

To ensure transparency and openness, we have adopted the collaboration policy developed for the Gaia Sprints. This policy requires that participants agree to the following:

All participants will be expected to openly share their ideas, expertise, code, and interim results. Project development will proceed out in the open, among participants and in the world.

Participants will be encouraged to change gears, start new collaborations, and combine projects. Any participant who contributes significantly to a project can expect co-authorship on resulting scientific papers, and any participant who gets significant contributions to a project is expected to include those contributors as co-authors.

These rules make it inadvisable to bring proprietary data sets or proprietary code, unless the participant bringing such assets has the rights to open them or add collaborators.
